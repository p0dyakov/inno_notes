5. Derivatives of Exponential Functions, The number e

Author

Zakhar Podyakov

Published

September 19, 2025

1. Summary

1.1 Introduction to Exponential Derivatives

The exploration of calculus often leads to moments of amazement. As mathematician Francois le Lionnais noted, “Who has not been amazed to learn that the function \(y = e^x\), like a phoenix rising again from its own ashes, is its own derivative?” This unique property makes the exponential function with base e fundamental in calculus.

This section focuses on understanding the derivatives of exponential functions, such as \(f(x) = 2^x\) or \(f(x) = 7^x\). The primary goal is to discover why the number e is so special and why \(e^x\) is considered the most important exponential function.

1.2 An Intuitive Approach: Population Growth

To build intuition, let’s consider a function \(M(t) = 2^t\), which represents the mass of a population of creatures that doubles every day.

  • At \(t=0\), the mass is \(2^0 = 1\).
  • At \(t=1\), the mass is \(2^1 = 2\).
  • At \(t=2\), the mass is \(2^2 = 4\).
  • At \(t=3\), the mass is \(2^3 = 8\).

The derivative, \(dM/dt\), represents the instantaneous rate of growth of this mass. If we approximate this rate over a full day, for instance from day 3 to day 4, the mass increases from 8 to 16. The rate of change is \((16 - 8) / (1 \text{ day}) = 8\) units of mass per day. Notice that this rate of growth (8) is equal to the population mass at the start of the day (\(2^3 = 8\)).

This observation might tempt us to conclude that the derivative of \(2^t\) is simply \(2^t\). However, this is just an approximation over a large time interval. Calculus requires us to analyze this for infinitesimally small intervals of time.

1.3 Formalizing the Derivative of Exponential Functions

The derivative of an exponential function \(a^t\) is defined by the limit: \[ \frac{d(a^t)}{dt} = \lim_{dt \to 0} \frac{a^{t+dt} - a^t}{dt} \]

Using the property of exponents, we can rewrite \(a^{t+dt}\) as \(a^t \cdot a^{dt}\). \[ \frac{d(a^t)}{dt} = \lim_{dt \to 0} \frac{a^t a^{dt} - a^t}{dt} \] Factoring out \(a^t\) from the numerator gives: \[ \frac{d(a^t)}{dt} = a^t \cdot \left( \lim_{dt \to 0} \frac{a^{dt} - 1}{dt} \right) \]

This reveals a crucial property: the derivative of an exponential function is proportional to the function itself. The term in the parentheses is a constant that depends only on the base a, not on the variable t.

1.4 The Proportionality Constant and The Natural Logarithm

The constant of proportionality is the value that \(\frac{a^{dt} - 1}{dt}\) approaches as \(dt\) gets closer to zero. By testing different bases a, we find:

  • For \(a=2\), the constant is approximately \(0.6931...\)
  • For \(a=3\), the constant is approximately \(1.0986...\)
  • For \(a=8\), the constant is approximately \(2.0794...\)

This mysterious constant is the natural logarithm of the base, denoted as \(\ln(a)\). Therefore, the general rule for the derivative of an exponential function is: \[ \frac{d}{dt}a^t = a^t \cdot \ln(a) \]

1.5 The Special Number \(e\)

A natural question arises: Is there a base a for which the proportionality constant, \(\ln(a)\), is exactly 1? The answer is yes, and this unique base is the number e.

The number e (approximately 2.71828…) is formally defined as the base for which the derivative of the function \(e^t\) is simply \(e^t\). \[ \frac{d}{dt}e^t = e^t \] This property makes e the most “natural” base for calculus because it simplifies derivative calculations significantly.

1.6 The Chain Rule and Changing Bases

The power of using base e becomes even more apparent with the chain rule. The derivative of a function like \(e^{ct}\) is: \[ \frac{d}{dt}(e^{ct}) = e^{ct} \cdot \frac{d}{dt}(ct) = c \cdot e^{ct} \] Any exponential function \(a^t\) can be rewritten using base e because \(a = e^{\ln(a)}\). \[ a^t = (e^{\ln(a)})^t = e^{(\ln a)t} \] Applying the chain rule to this form confirms our general derivative rule: \[ \frac{d}{dt}(e^{(\ln a)t}) = e^{(\ln a)t} \cdot \ln(a) = a^t \cdot \ln(a) \]

2. Definitions

  • Exponential Function: A function in the form \(f(x) = a^x\), where ‘a’ is a positive constant (the base) and the variable ‘x’ is the exponent.
  • Derivative: Represents the instantaneous rate of change of a function. For a function \(f(x)\), its derivative \(f'(x)\) gives the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(f(x)\) at any point \(x\).
  • The number e (Euler’s Number): A special mathematical constant approximately equal to 2.71828. It is the unique base for which the derivative of the exponential function \(e^x\) is itself, \(e^x\).
  • Natural Logarithm (ln): The logarithm with base e. The natural logarithm of a number \(x\), written as \(\ln(x)\), is the power to which e must be raised to equal \(x\). It is the inverse of the function \(e^x\).

3. Formulas

  • General Derivative of an Exponential Function: \(\frac{d}{dx}a^x = a^x \ln(a)\)
  • Derivative of the Natural Exponential Function: \(\frac{d}{dx}e^x = e^x\)
  • Chain Rule for Exponential Functions: \(\frac{d}{dx}e^{u(x)} = e^{u(x)} \cdot u'(x)\)
  • General Chain Rule for Exponentials: \(\frac{d}{dx}a^{u(x)} = a^{u(x)} \ln(a) \cdot u'(x)\)
  • Changing Base Formula: \(a^x = e^{x \ln(a)}\)

4. Mistakes

  • Confusing the Power Rule with the Exponential Rule: A common mistake is to apply the power rule to \(d/dx(a^x)\) and incorrectly get \(x \cdot a^{x-1}\). Why it’s wrong: The power rule, \(d/dx(x^n) = nx^{n-1}\), applies when the base is a variable and the exponent is a constant. The exponential rule applies when the base is a constant and the exponent is a variable.
  • Forgetting the \(\ln(a)\) Factor: Assuming the derivative of any exponential function \(a^x\) is just \(a^x\). Why it’s wrong: This is only true for the specific base e. For any other base a, you must multiply by the natural logarithm of that base, \(\ln(a)\).
  • Ignoring the Chain Rule: When differentiating a composite function like \(e^{2x}\), stating the derivative is \(e^{2x}\) instead of \(2e^{2x}\). Why it’s wrong: The chain rule requires you to also multiply by the derivative of the inner function (in this case, \(2x\)), which is 2.
  • Incorrectly Applying the Chain Rule: For a function like \(5^{x^2}\), incorrectly calculating the derivative as \(x^2 \ln(5) \cdot 5^{x^2-1}\). Why it’s wrong: This mixes up the power rule and the exponential rule. The correct derivative is \(5^{x^2} \ln(5) \cdot (2x)\).

5. Examples

5.1 Basic Exponential Derivative
Question: Find the derivative of \(f(x) = 7^x\).
Click to see the solution
  1. Identify the rule: The function is in the form \(a^x\), so we use the rule \(\frac{d}{dx}a^x = a^x \ln(a)\).
  2. Apply the rule: Here, \(a=7\). \[ f'(x) = 7^x \ln(7) \]
Answer: The derivative is \(7^x \ln(7)\).
5.2 Derivative of the Natural Exponential
Question: What is the derivative of \(g(t) = e^t\)?
Click to see the solution
  1. Identify the rule: This is the special case where the base is e. The rule is \(\frac{d}{dt}e^t = e^t\).
  2. Apply the rule: \[ g'(t) = e^t \]
Answer: The derivative is \(e^t\).
5.3 Exponential with a Constant Multiplier
Question: Differentiate \(y = 5 \cdot 3^x\).
Click to see the solution
  1. Use the constant multiple rule: The constant 5 can be factored out of the differentiation process. \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 5 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(3^x) \]
  2. Differentiate the exponential part: Use the rule \(\frac{d}{dx}a^x = a^x \ln(a)\) with \(a=3\). \[ \frac{d}{dx}(3^x) = 3^x \ln(3) \]
  3. Combine the results: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 5 \cdot 3^x \ln(3) \]
Answer: The derivative is \(5 \cdot 3^x \ln(3)\).
5.4 Chain Rule with Base e
Question: Find the derivative of \(h(x) = e^{5x}\).
Click to see the solution
  1. Identify the inner and outer functions: The outer function is \(e^u\) and the inner function is \(u = 5x\).
  2. Apply the chain rule: The derivative is (derivative of outer function) \(\times\) (derivative of inner function).
    • Derivative of \(e^u\) is \(e^u\), which is \(e^{5x}\).
    • Derivative of \(5x\) is \(5\).
  3. Multiply them together: \[ h'(x) = e^{5x} \cdot 5 = 5e^{5x} \]
Answer: The derivative is \(5e^{5x}\).
5.5 Chain Rule with a General Base
Question: Find the derivative of \(f(t) = 10^{t^2}\).
Click to see the solution
  1. Identify the rule and functions: This requires the general exponential rule and the chain rule. The outer function is \(10^u\) and the inner function is \(u = t^2\).
  2. Apply the rules: The derivative is (derivative of outer) \(\times\) (derivative of inner).
    • Derivative of \(10^u\) is \(10^u \ln(10)\), which is \(10^{t^2} \ln(10)\).
    • Derivative of \(t^2\) is \(2t\).
  3. Multiply the results: \[ f'(t) = 10^{t^2} \ln(10) \cdot (2t) \]
Answer: The derivative is \(2t \cdot 10^{t^2} \ln(10)\).
5.6 Finding the Slope of a Tangent Line
Question: What is the slope of the tangent line to the curve \(y = e^x\) at the point \((1, e)\)?
Click to see the solution
  1. Find the derivative: The derivative of \(e^x\) gives the slope at any point \(x\). \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = e^x \]
  2. Evaluate the derivative at the given x-value: We need the slope at \(x=1\). \[ \text{Slope} = e^1 = e \]
Answer: The slope of the tangent line at \((1, e)\) is e.
5.7 Application Problem
Question: A bacterial culture starts with 100 bacteria and doubles every hour. The population can be modeled by the function \(P(t) = 100 \cdot 2^t\), where \(t\) is in hours. At what rate is the population growing at \(t=3\) hours?
Click to see the solution
  1. Find the derivative of the population function: We need to find \(P'(t)\). \[ P'(t) = \frac{d}{dt}(100 \cdot 2^t) = 100 \cdot \frac{d}{dt}(2^t) \] \[ P'(t) = 100 \cdot (2^t \ln(2)) \]
  2. Evaluate the derivative at \(t=3\): \[ P'(3) = 100 \cdot (2^3 \ln(2)) \] \[ P'(3) = 100 \cdot (8 \ln(2)) = 800 \ln(2) \]
  3. Calculate the approximate value: \(\ln(2) \approx 0.693\). \[ P'(3) \approx 800 \cdot 0.693 \approx 554.4 \]
Answer: After 3 hours, the population is growing at a rate of approximately 554 bacteria per hour.
